Dryer Vents & Fire Safety

A dryer that runs but takes longer than usual to dry clothes is not just inconvenient; it's a warning sign. Restricted exhaust vents trap heat, force the dryer to work harder, and create the conditions for lint to ignite.

⚠️ What the numbers say

The U.S. Fire Administration reports approximately 2,900 residential dryer fires per year, 34% caused by failure to clean the dryer or venting. In Southern California's dusty climate, lint accumulates faster than most homeowners expect.

Signs your vent needs attention: clothes take more than one cycle to dry, the dryer exterior is very hot to the touch, the laundry room feels humid, or it has been more than a year since the duct was cleaned.

Every service visit includes a vent inspection at no extra charge. If we find a restriction, we'll clear it and confirm proper airflow before we leave.

Repair or Replace Your Dryer?

Dryers typically last 13 years. If your machine is under 10 years old, repair is almost always the right call; a quality replacement dryer runs $600–$1,000 installed, and common repairs cost a fraction of that. Even a heating element replacement on a 7-year-old dryer makes economic sense. For a full breakdown by symptom, see our Orange County appliance repair cost guide.

Our rule of thumb: If the repair cost is under 50% of a comparable new dryer and the machine is under 10 years old, fix it. If it's over 12 years old and needs a motor, drum bearing, or control board, we'll tell you honestly, and help you decide rather than push for the repair.

A dryer that runs two or three cycles to dry one load almost always has a restricted exhaust vent. Lint builds up in the duct over time, cutting airflow and forcing the dryer to work longer. Squealing usually means worn drum support rollers or a failing idler pulley; both are standard wear items on dryers over five years old. A rhythmic thumping is typically worn drum glides or a deteriorated felt drum seal. These parts are inexpensive and most are fixed in a single visit.
Premature shut-off is usually a faulty moisture sensor bar or a hi-limit thermostat tripping from overheating.
