Control Boards, F-Codes, and GE's Known Weak Points

GE appliances fail in specific, recognizable ways. These are the repairs our technicians handle most often on Orange County homes.

Refrigerator Main Control Board Failure The board that runs the whole fridge fails, and the compressor or fans never get the signal to run, so the unit stops cooling. This is GE's best-known weak point across many models. We test the board before condemning the compressor.
Refrigerator Not Cooling / Defrost Fault A warm fridge can also be a stuck defrost system icing over the evaporator coils or a worn evaporator fan. We diagnose the sealed system, defrost, and fan precisely rather than guessing at the most expensive part first.
Ice Maker Not Working A GE ice maker that stops filling is often the water inlet valve, the ice-maker assembly, or a frozen fill tube, not the whole door. We test the valve and assembly so you are not paying to replace a part that still works.
Oven F-Code or Won't Heat An F-code is the electronic control reporting a fault, usually a failed board or a shorted temperature sensor. A cold oven often means a dead bake element or a weak igniter. We meter the element, sensor, and board to find the real cause.
Washer Won't Agitate or Spin On GE top-loaders, a failed mode shifter, worn motor, broken lid switch, or transmission fault stops the agitate or spin. Older direct-drive GE washers are known for mode-shifter trouble. We test each part before quoting.
Dryer Won't Heat Tumbles but blows cold. On electric models it is a blown thermal fuse or heating element; on gas it is the igniter or valve coil. A blocked vent overheats and blows the fuse, so we check airflow as part of every no-heat repair.
Dishwasher Won't Drain or Clean Standing water or gritty dishes usually trace to a clogged filter, a jammed drain pump, or a failed control board. We clear the blockage and confirm the pump and inlet valve before returning the unit to service.
Range Igniter or Bake Element A gas burner that clicks but won't light or an oven that won't reach temperature usually means a weak igniter, a failed bake element, or a bad spark module. We meter the igniter and element to pin down which one before quoting parts.

Hotpoint to Monogram: The GE Family Explained

GE is not one line of appliances, it is five, and knowing which tier you own helps explain both the price you paid and the parts your repair needs. From the entry level up, GE Appliances builds Hotpoint (budget), GE (mainstream), GE Profile (upper-mid with more features), Cafe (design-forward with customizable hardware), and Monogram (built-in luxury that competes with Sub-Zero and Wolf). GE Appliances has been owned by Haier since 2016, but the brands, the engineering, and the Louisville plants carried straight through.

What matters for a repair is that these tiers share a great deal under the panel. A Cafe range and a GE Profile range are close cousins, and a Monogram built-in refrigerator uses sealed-system and control parts you will recognize from the mainstream line. So a technician who genuinely knows GE already knows most of the family, and the part that fixes a GE fridge is often the exact part that fixes the Profile next to it. We carry the common boards, fans, valves, and elements on the van precisely because they cross so many GE models.

There is one thread that runs through the whole family, and it is worth knowing: GE's electronic control boards. The main control board in the refrigerator and the electronic oven control in the range are the parts GE appliances are most known for failing. The good news is they are a well-understood, well-stocked repair. If your GE, Profile, Cafe, or Monogram appliance is acting erratically, losing cooling, or throwing an F-code, read us the model number when you call and we will bring the right board and the right diagnostic plan so the first visit is the visit that fixes it.
