Common Washing Machine Problems We Fix

Our technicians diagnose and repair all washer failure modes — usually in a single visit.

● Washer not spinning or drum won't turn
● Leaking water from door, bottom, or back
● Washer won't drain — clothes soaking wet
● Violent shaking or banging during spin cycle
● Loud grinding, rumbling, or squealing noise
● Washer won't start or power light is dead
● Not filling with water or filling too slowly
● Error codes on display (UE, LE, 4E, 5E, F21)
● Musty smell or mould inside drum
● Door latch won't lock or won't release

Should You Repair or Replace Your Washing Machine?

Washers typically last 11–14 years. If your machine is under 10 years old, repair almost always makes financial sense — a new mid-range washer runs $700–$1,200, and installation adds another $100–$200. For machines over 12 years old facing a major component failure (motor, drum bearing, control board), replacement may be the better call. Washing Machine Repair — FAQs

A washer that won't spin usually has one of three causes: a worn or broken drive belt, a failed lid switch (top-loaders) or door latch (front-loaders), or a faulty motor coupling. Overloading can also cause the machine to skip the spin cycle as a safety measure. A technician can pinpoint the exact part in one visit.
Leaks from a washing machine usually come from a worn door boot seal (front-loaders), a cracked or loose drain hose, a failed water inlet valve, or a damaged pump. Using too much detergent can also cause overflow through the door. Front-load leaks from the bottom are often the drain pump or hose connection.
A washer that won't drain is almost always caused by a clogged drain pump filter (coins, lint, or small garments get lodged inside), a blocked drain hose, or a failed drain pump motor. Check your pockets before every load — a single coin is the most common culprit in OC households.
Violent shaking during the spin cycle usually means the machine is unlevel, the load is unbalanced, or the shock absorbers and suspension rods have worn out. On older front-loaders, worn drum bearings cause both shaking and a loud rumbling noise. Leveling the machine and redistributing the load are quick DIY checks; bearing or absorber replacements need a technician.
