Blink Codes, Control Boards, and KitchenAid's Known Weak Points

KitchenAid appliances fail in specific, recognizable ways. These are the repairs our technicians handle most often on Orange County homes.

Dishwasher Won't Drain Standing water at the bottom usually traces to a clogged filter, a jammed drain pump, or a blocked drain hose or air gap. We clear the blockage and confirm the pump and check valve before returning the unit to service.
Dishwasher Won't Clean Gritty or wet dishes point to the wash pump, the spray arms, the chopper, or the diverter that routes water between racks. We check each so the dishwasher actually cleans, not just runs.
Dishwasher Clean-Light Blinking A flashing clean light is a stored fault, and the blink count maps to a specific error, most often a heating-circuit or thermistor problem or a control-board fault. We read the code and confirm it with a meter before repairing.
Oven F-Code or Won't Heat An F2 or F3 code is the electronic control reporting a failed board or a shorted temperature sensor. A cold oven often means a dead bake element or a weak igniter. We meter the element, sensor, and board to find the real cause.
Cooktop Won't Ignite or Heat A gas burner that clicks without lighting, or an induction zone that stays cold, usually means a failed igniter, spark module, or element. We test the ignition and element to pin down which part failed.
Refrigerator Not Cooling A warm KitchenAid fridge points to a failed evaporator fan, a stuck defrost system, a control board, or a sealed-system fault. On built-ins we diagnose carefully because the repair is worth doing on such an expensive unit.
Ice Maker Not Working A KitchenAid ice maker that stops filling is often the water inlet valve, the ice-maker assembly, or a frozen fill tube, not the whole unit. We test the valve and assembly so you do not pay to replace a part that still works.
Disposal Humming or Leaking A KitchenAid disposal that hums without spinning is usually jammed, while a leak is a worn seal or a cracked body. We free the jam and repair the seal, or replace the unit the same day when it is beyond repair.

KitchenAid: Whirlpool's Premium Kitchen Line

KitchenAid has a premium reputation, and it earns it, but it is worth knowing where the brand sits. KitchenAid is owned and built by Whirlpool Corporation, the same company behind Whirlpool, Maytag, and Amana. KitchenAid is the premium kitchen line of that family: the same underlying platforms and many of the same parts, dressed in better finishes, quieter dishwasher motors, and stronger cooking performance. So a KitchenAid dishwasher shares much of its mechanism with a Whirlpool one, and a KitchenAid oven uses the same family of electronic controls.

That connection is good news when something breaks. A technician who genuinely knows Whirlpool already knows most of KitchenAid, and the drain pump, control board, or bake element that fixes your KitchenAid is often the exact part that fixes its Whirlpool cousin. We carry those common parts on the van precisely because they cross so many models, which usually means a faster, one-visit repair on a premium machine that would otherwise be slow and expensive to source parts for.

It also changes the repair-or-replace math in your favor. KitchenAid units, especially built-in dishwashers, wall ovens, and refrigerators, are expensive to replace, while the common faults cost a fraction of that and use widely available Whirlpool-family parts. So repair is usually the smart money. If you are not sure exactly which KitchenAid model you own, read us the number off the door frame or the tub when you call, and we will bring the right parts the first time.
