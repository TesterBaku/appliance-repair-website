Common Freezer Problems We Fix

Most freezer failures are diagnosed and repaired in a single visit. Common parts stocked on every service vehicle.

●Freezer not freezing: running but not getting cold
●Excessive frost or ice buildup inside the freezer
●Freezer temperature fluctuating or too warm
●Loud humming, buzzing, rattling, or clicking noises
●Freezer running constantly without cycling off
●Door seal worn, cracked, or not closing properly
●Water pooling under the unit or inside the freezer
●Freezer won't turn on or tripped breaker on startup
●Ice maker not making ice or producing misshapen cubes
●Control panel error codes or unresponsive display

Upright Freezers

Repair or Replace Your Freezer?

Standalone freezers last 10–20 years with reasonable care. For a unit under 10 years old, most repairs make clear financial sense: a quality chest or upright freezer runs $300–$800 new, and most common repairs are well under that. For premium built-in brands like Sub-Zero, the math extends even further: a replacement column freezer starts at $3,000+. We give you the numbers before any work starts so you can decide.

Our guideline: If the freezer is under 12 years old and the repair is under 50% of a comparable replacement, fix it. If the compressor has failed on a 15-year-old chest freezer, we'll say so honestly, and we won't charge you a full repair bill just to deliver that news.

Freezer Repair: Frequently Asked Questions

A freezer that runs but won't get cold usually has one of four causes: a failed compressor, a faulty evaporator fan motor (cold air isn't circulating), a defrost thermostat failure (ice has blocked airflow over the coils), or a refrigerant leak. A technician can diagnose the exact cause in one visit using temperature and pressure checks.
Frost buildup almost always comes from warm, moist air entering through a damaged door gasket that no longer seals properly. A failed defrost heater or defrost timer can also cause frost to accumulate on the evaporator coils. All of these are repairable in a single visit.
A loud hum or buzz usually points to the condenser fan motor or compressor working harder than normal, often from dirty condenser coils. Rattling or vibrating is commonly a loose drain pan or an unlevel unit. A clicking sound every few minutes is often the compressor trying to start and failing; this needs attention before the compressor fails completely.
